Process for Finding Adulteration

Step 1: Collecting Samples Step 2: Laboratory Analysis Step 3: Interpretation of Results Detection of Food Adulterants

Step 1: Collecting Samples

Collecting samples of food is a critical preliminary phase in identifying adulteration. This process involves gathering representative samples from various batches or sources of the food product under scrutiny. Adhering to stringent sampling protocols is paramount to ensure the accuracy and reliability of subsequent analyses. These protocols encompass elements such as random selection, proper labeling, and maintaining optimal storage conditions to avert contamination and uphold the integrity of the samples.

Step 2: Laboratory Analysis

Laboratory analysis is vital in detecting food adulteration. It involves rigorous examination of samples using techniques like chromatography, spectroscopy, and microbiological assays. Chromatography separates components, spectroscopy identifies chemical composition, and microbiological assays detect pathogens. This meticulous analysis ensures food safety and authenticity, protecting public health and consumer trust.

Step 3: Interpretation of Results

Interpreting results in food analysis is crucial for determining safety and quality. After conducting tests, data is analyzed against standards. Statistical techniques like mean comparison help assess differences, while expert judgment contextualizes findings. Accurate interpretation informs decisions on product safety, compliance, and public health.

Detection of Food Adulterants

1.Fruits and Vegetables:

Method 1:
1. Take a cotton piece soaked in water or vegetable oil. (conduct the test separately).
2. Rub the outer green surface of a small part of green vegetable/chilli.
3. If the cotton turns green, then it is adulterated with malachite green.

Method 2:


1. Take a small part of the sample and place on a piece of moistened white blotting paper.


2. The impression of colour on the paper indicates the use of malachite green, or any other low priced artificial colour.

Test 2:

Detection of artificial colour on green peas

Testing Method:

1. Take little amount of green peas in a transparent glass.

2. Add water to it and mix well.

3. Let it stand for half an hour.

4. Clear separation of colour in water indicates adulteration.

Test 3:

Detection of rhodamine B in sweet potato:

Testing Method:

1. Take a cotton ball soaked in water or vegetable oil. (conduct the test separately).

2. Rub the outer red surface of the sweet potato.

3. If cotton absorbs colour, then it indicates the usage of rhodamine B for colouring the outer surface of sweet potato.

2. Food grains and it's products:

Test 1:

Detection of extraneous matter (dust, pebble, stone, straw, weed seeds, damaged grains, weevilled grain, insects, rodent hair and excreta) in food grains:

Testing Method:

1. Take small quantity of sample in a glass plate.

2. Examine the impurities visually.

3. Pure food grains will not have any such impurities.

4. Impurities are observed visually in adulterated food grains.
